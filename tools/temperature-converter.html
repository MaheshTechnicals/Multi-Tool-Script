Supported Temperature Scales

Celsius (°C)

The most widely used temperature scale globally, based on the freezing point (0°C) and boiling point (100°C) of water at standard atmospheric pressure. Used in most countries and scientific applications.

  • Freezing Point: 0°C
  • Boiling Point: 100°C
  • Absolute Zero: -273.15°C
  • Usage: Worldwide standard, scientific research

Fahrenheit (°F)

Primarily used in the United States, based on a scale where water freezes at 32°F and boils at 212°F. Named after physicist Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit.

  • Freezing Point: 32°F
  • Boiling Point: 212°F
  • Absolute Zero: -459.67°F
  • Usage: United States, some Caribbean countries

Kelvin (K)

The base unit of thermodynamic temperature in the International System of Units (SI). Starts at absolute zero, making it essential for scientific calculations.

  • Freezing Point: 273.15 K
  • Boiling Point: 373.15 K
  • Absolute Zero: 0 K
  • Usage: Scientific research, physics, chemistry

Rankine (°R)

An absolute temperature scale similar to Kelvin but using Fahrenheit-sized degrees. Used primarily in engineering applications in the United States.

  • Freezing Point: 491.67°R
  • Boiling Point: 671.67°R
  • Absolute Zero: 0°R
  • Usage: Engineering, thermodynamics

Réaumur (°Ré)

A historical temperature scale where water freezes at 0°Ré and boils at 80°Ré. Rarely used today but still found in some European contexts.

  • Freezing Point: 0°Ré
  • Boiling Point: 80°Ré
  • Absolute Zero: -218.52°Ré
  • Usage: Historical, some European applications

Temperature Conversion Guide

Understanding Temperature Scales

Temperature scales are systems for measuring thermal energy. Each scale has its own zero point and unit size, making conversions necessary when working across different systems or regions.

When to Use Each Scale

  • Celsius: Daily weather, cooking, most scientific work
  • Fahrenheit: US weather reports, medical temperatures
  • Kelvin: Scientific calculations, physics, chemistry
  • Rankine: Engineering thermodynamics, US scientific work
  • Réaumur: Historical contexts, some European applications

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are there different temperature scales?

Different temperature scales were developed for various purposes and regions. Celsius is based on water's properties, Fahrenheit was designed for human comfort ranges, and Kelvin starts at absolute zero for scientific calculations.

Which temperature scale is most accurate?

All temperature scales are equally accurate when properly calibrated. The choice depends on your application - Kelvin for science, Celsius for most international use, and Fahrenheit in the United States.

Can I convert negative temperatures?

Yes! Our converter handles all temperature ranges, including negative values. This is especially useful for scientific applications and extreme weather conditions.

Conversion Formulas

°C to °F: (°C × 9/5) + 32
°F to °C: (°F - 32) × 5/9
°C to K: °C + 273.15
K to °C: K - 273.15
°F to °R: °F + 459.67
°C to °Ré: °C × 4/5
